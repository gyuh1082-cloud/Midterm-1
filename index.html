Inclusive Harlem: Redesigning Harlem Pedestrian Plaza for Comfort, Dignity, and Shared Public Use

This project proposes a more welcoming, accessible, and inclusive plaza for a wide mix of users. The redesign focuses on comfort, dignity, visibility, circulation, and shared public use, using AI-assisted image development and human design judgment.

About the Project

Project Goal: Redesign Harlem Pedestrian Plaza as a more inclusive public space that works for many different users, with stronger comfort, accessibility, dignity, visibility, and shared use.

Essential Question: How can Harlem Pedestrian Plaza be redesigned so it feels more welcoming, inclusive, and usable for a wide mix of people with different needs?

The Problem

Harlem Plaza does not function as an equally inclusive public space. While it is active and well-used, its current design conditions systematically limit how different groups are able to occupy, move through, and remain in the space. These limitations are not accidental — they are spatial decisions that prioritize certain users over others.

An inclusive public plaza must do more than exist as open space. It must actively support different bodies, time patterns, and social needs. The current plaza falls short of that standard in several key ways:

  • Elderly residents: The existing seating consists primarily of granite seat walls and fixed benches without armrests. For elderly users, rising from a low stone surface without arm support is physically difficult. This is not just discomfort — it limits how long seniors can remain in the space, effectively excluding them from extended use.
  • People with disabilities: While entry points meet ADA requirements, the interior of the plaza lacks continuous accessible pathways. Key areas such as the central lawn and upper sections are difficult to reach for wheelchair users and those with mobility limitations. Partial accessibility signals that not all users are equally considered.
  • Families with children: The plaza provides open space but lacks clearly defined, safe play areas with visible sightlines. Parents are forced to either stand in full sun or sit in areas that do not face their children. This creates discomfort and limits how families can use the space.
  • Unhoused individuals: Public plazas in dense urban neighborhoods are often the primary outdoor space for people experiencing homelessness. However, many public spaces are designed in ways that intentionally discourage their presence. Features such as segmented benches, sloped seating, and uncomfortable materials are often framed as neutral design decisions but function as forms of exclusion.
  • In Harlem Plaza, the absence of weather protection, limited access to water, and lack of amenities that support longer stays create conditions that are not actively hostile, but still exclude through neglect. An inclusive redesign must move beyond avoiding harm and instead provide spaces that support dignity, rest, and basic human needs.
  • Commuters and pass-through users: Heavy pedestrian traffic from the nearby 1-train creates fast-moving circulation that overlaps with areas intended for sitting and gathering. Without clear spatial separation, movement and rest conflict with one another, reducing comfort for both.

The challenge of this redesign is not simply aesthetic. It is spatial and social: how can one plaza serve many users fairly, without making any group feel excluded, displaced, or overlooked?

The Problem

Harlem Plaza does not fully support inclusive, dignified public use. Existing conditions suggest limited comfort, insufficient shade, uneven accessibility, and a layout that does not clearly balance different types of users or activities. As a result, the plaza can feel under-equipped for elderly residents, people with disabilities, families, commuters, and people who need places to sit, rest, or gather comfortably.

The redesign challenge is not only aesthetic. It is social and spatial: how can one plaza serve many people fairly without making any group feel excluded, displaced, or overlooked?

Site / Context

This project focuses on inclusion as a site-analysis lens. The plaza was studied through comfort, accessibility, circulation, visibility, and shared use. The goal is to understand how the current space supports or fails different users throughout the day.

User Groups

  • Elderly residents
  • People with disabilities
  • Families and children
  • Commuters and pass-through users
  • Unhoused individuals
  • Local community members

Observed Issues

  • Uneven comfort and limited weather protection
  • Unclear movement paths and weak seating variety
  • Potential conflict between active and quiet uses
  • Accessibility gaps in how the space is experienced
  • Limited amenities that support dignity and longer stays

The plaza was analyzed through direct observation of how different users occupy space throughout the day. Patterns of comfort, movement, and exclusion reveal that the design does not distribute usability evenly.

Comfort and Inclusion: The most comfortable areas of the plaza are limited and unevenly distributed. Shaded seating is scarce, and many seating options lack backrests or arm support. As a result, users who require more physical support — including elderly individuals and people with mobility challenges — are less able to remain in the space for extended periods.

Accessibility: While entrances meet accessibility standards, interior circulation is fragmented. Not all areas are equally reachable, particularly for wheelchair users. This creates a hierarchy of access where some spaces are effectively restricted despite being visually open.

Circulation and Conflict: Movement through the plaza is dominated by commuters passing to and from the subway. These fast-moving paths overlap with areas intended for sitting and gathering, creating friction between users who are moving quickly and those who want to remain in place.

Visibility and Safety: The lack of clearly defined zones means that users must constantly negotiate space. Families lack clear areas where children can play safely within view, and individuals seeking rest or privacy have few protected zones.

Dignity and Duration of Stay: The plaza is designed for short-term use rather than extended occupation. Limited shade, lack of amenities, and rigid seating reduce the ability for users — especially unhoused individuals — to remain comfortably and with dignity.

Comfort and Inclusion Analysis

A spatial analysis of the plaza identifies clear patterns of comfort and exclusion. The central paved area is fully exposed to direct sun from approximately 10am to 4pm during warmer months, creating a thermal barrier that elderly users, young children, and individuals with heat sensitivity cannot comfortably cross. Seating along the eastern edge receives partial afternoon shade, while northern seating areas remain fully exposed throughout the day. As a result, the most accessible routes through the plaza are not the most comfortable, and the most comfortable areas are limited in availability.

The primary accessible path runs along the southern edge from the subway entrance, but there is no continuous accessible route connecting to the upper northwestern section of the plaza. This effectively limits full access to a significant portion of the space, reinforcing uneven usability across different user groups.

Conflict Analysis

The primary use conflict occurs between fast-moving transit commuters and stationary users. During peak hours (approximately 7–9am and 5–7pm), commuters use the plaza as a direct route between the subway entrance and surrounding streets. The fastest movement path cuts diagonally across the central plaza — the same area most suitable for seating, gathering, and social use.

Secondary conflicts occur between active and quiet users. Children and informal play activities require open, flexible space, while elderly users and individuals seeking rest need calm, stable environments. The current layout does not clearly separate these uses, forcing different activity types into shared space without defined boundaries.

Accessibility Review

Current accessibility conditions are partially effective but incomplete. The main entry from the 137th Street subway stop meets ADA standards in terms of width and surface quality. However, several key limitations remain:

  • The central lawn area includes grade changes without clearly integrated ramps or level circulation paths, limiting access for wheelchair users.
  • Fixed granite seating is low and lacks armrests, making it difficult for elderly users or those with mobility limitations to use comfortably.
  • The plaza lacks tactile paving or surface indicators for low-vision users navigating interior spaces.
  • Wayfinding and signage are minimal and primarily English-based, limiting accessibility for non-English-speaking users.

Designing for Unhoused Users

Designing for unhoused individuals requires confronting a broader pattern in public space design: what is often presented as “neutral” or “inclusive” design has historically included features intended to discourage long-term presence. Armrests placed at the center of benches, sloped or segmented seating, and deliberately uncomfortable surfaces are commonly used to prevent lying down or extended rest. These are not neutral decisions — they are forms of spatial exclusion often referred to as hostile design.

This proposal explicitly rejects that approach. Inclusion in public space does not mean managing or hiding vulnerable users; it means designing environments that support a wider range of human needs without stigma or displacement.

Genuinely inclusive design for unhoused users includes:

  • Covered seating and weather protection: Shade structures and rain shelter support all users, but are especially important for individuals who cannot leave the space during extreme weather conditions.
  • Access to water: Drinking fountains or bottle-filling stations are basic public amenities that support health and dignity, particularly during periods of high heat.
  • 24-hour usability: A plaza that remains safely lit and welcoming at night serves a broader range of users, including those who rely on public space beyond daytime hours.
  • Absence of hostile features: Seating should not include center armrest dividers, sloped surfaces, or materials designed to discourage rest. These elements are often framed as design solutions but function as exclusionary barriers.

This approach aligns with a broader body of urban design thinking that prioritizes inclusion and dignity in public space. Researchers and practitioners such as William H. Whyte and Mitchell Duneier have argued that successful public spaces are those that support a wide range of users, including those who are most vulnerable, rather than designing them out.

Intellectual Context

This proposal builds on a broader body of urban design and social research that examines how public spaces succeed or fail based on who they serve. The argument that inclusion requires designing for the most vulnerable users is not only a design preference, but a position supported by decades of research and observation.

William H. Whyte, The Social Life of Small Urban Spaces (1980): Whyte’s observational studies of plazas and public spaces demonstrated that successful spaces attract a wide diversity of users. He emphasized that spaces become more vibrant and functional when they support a range of activities and users, including those who rely on public space for extended periods of time.

Mitchell Duneier, Sidewalk (1999): Duneier’s ethnographic work documents the lives of unhoused individuals in New York City and argues that they contribute to the social and economic life of public space. His research challenges the idea that public space should exclude vulnerable users, and instead frames inclusion as a matter of social responsibility.

Hostile Design and Public Space: Contemporary research and documentation have shown that many public space “improvements” are designed to discourage certain users. Features such as divided benches, sloped seating, and anti-sleep infrastructure are often presented as neutral design choices, but function as mechanisms of exclusion. This proposal explicitly rejects these strategies and instead prioritizes non-hostile, inclusive design.

NYC Universal Design Guidelines: New York City’s Universal Design guidelines extend beyond minimum ADA compliance to address a wider range of users, including elderly individuals, people with mobility limitations, and users with sensory or cognitive needs. This proposal aligns with those principles by emphasizing continuous accessibility, seating variety, and inclusive wayfinding.

Together, these sources reinforce the central argument of this project: that inclusive public space is not achieved by removing conflict or excluding users, but by designing environments that support a wide range of needs, including those of the most vulnerable.

Design Options

Three concepts were developed to test different approaches to inclusion in the plaza. Each option responds to the same core question, but emphasizes a different design priority.

Option 1 — Comfort First Plaza

This option prioritizes physical comfort as the primary driver of inclusion. It introduces shaded seating, rest zones, and benches with backs and armrests to support longer stays and reduce thermal discomfort. These improvements benefit the broadest range of users, including elderly residents, families, and commuters needing places to pause.

Limitation: While comfort improvements are essential, they do not fully address the equity and dignity dimension of the plaza. A more comfortable space can still subtly exclude certain users if it does not address accessibility gaps, circulation conflicts, or the needs of unhoused individuals. Comfort is necessary, but on its own it is not sufficient for true inclusion.

Option 2 — Shared Use Plaza

This option focuses on organizing the plaza to better accommodate different user groups simultaneously. It strengthens circulation paths, separates movement from gathering areas, and creates zones for active and quiet uses. This approach reduces conflict between commuters, families, and individuals seeking rest.

Limitation: While spatial zoning can reduce conflict, it can also reinforce separation between user groups. Assigning areas for specific activities may unintentionally signal where different users “belong,” rather than fostering a space where diverse uses overlap naturally. Shared use through separation is not the same as shared use through coexistence.

Option 3 — Community Support Plaza

This option centers dignity, care, and inclusive public-space principles. It treats the plaza as a social commons that supports a wide range of users, including those with the greatest needs. The design emphasizes welcoming edges, flexible gathering areas, and amenities that enable longer, more comfortable stays for all users.

Limitation: While this approach establishes the strongest values, concepts like “dignity” and “care” can remain abstract without specific physical design strategies. Without clear implementation, these ideas risk becoming aspirational rather than actionable.

Why This Option Is Selected: Despite its challenges, this option provides the most complete response to the project’s core goal of inclusion. The final proposal builds on this approach by translating its values into specific design elements — such as shaded seating, accessible circulation, supportive amenities, and non-hostile design choices — that create measurable improvements in comfort, accessibility, and dignity for different user groups.

Final Proposal

The final proposal combines the strongest elements of the three design options, using the Community Support approach as its foundation and translating its values into specific physical design features. The goal is to create a plaza that is not only more comfortable, but measurably more accessible, legible, and inclusive for a wide range of users.

Entry and Accessible Circulation: The redesigned plaza is entered from the 137th Street subway exit through a clearly marked, continuous accessible path that runs through the full length of the site. This path avoids abrupt grade changes, narrow passages, or surface transitions that would require assistance. It connects all major zones of the plaza, including the upper sections that are currently difficult to access, ensuring that no area is partially excluded.

Seating Strategy and Distribution: Seating is organized in clusters placed at regular intervals (approximately every 100–150 feet) along primary paths. Each cluster includes a mix of seating types: at least one bench with back support and armrests for elderly users, and additional movable chairs that allow users to adjust orientation and social distance. This ensures that users are never far from a place to rest, and that seating accommodates different physical needs and preferences.

Central Gathering and Weather Protection: In the central plaza area, three tensile shade structures provide consistent sun protection during the day and rain coverage throughout the year. These structures define a primary gathering zone without enclosing it. Within this space, full-length benches without center dividers allow for flexible use, including extended sitting or resting. A dual-height drinking fountain — accessible to both wheelchair users and standing adults — is placed centrally to provide a basic public amenity that supports health and dignity.

Lighting and Night Use: The plaza is designed to remain usable beyond daytime hours through evenly distributed, warm-toned (approximately 2700K) pedestrian-scale lighting. Pathways, seating areas, and edges are all illuminated without creating harsh or overexposed zones. The goal is to create a space that feels safe and welcoming at night rather than empty or defensive.

Non-Hostile Design Approach: The proposal explicitly avoids hostile design features. Seating does not include central armrest dividers intended to prevent lying down, surfaces are not deliberately uncomfortable, and edges are not designed to discourage occupation. Instead, the plaza supports a wider range of uses and durations of stay, recognizing that public space must serve users with different needs, including those without access to private space.

Language and Community Representation: All wayfinding and interpretive signage is provided in both English and Spanish, reflecting the linguistic reality of the surrounding community. This establishes bilingual communication as a baseline condition of accessibility rather than an added feature.

Core Design Features

  • Distributed seating clusters with varied seating types
  • Central shaded gathering zone with weather protection

  • Explicit avoidance of hostile design features

Budget and Implementation Strategy

The proposed redesign is organized into phased interventions that prioritize high-impact improvements to accessibility, comfort, and dignity. The cost structure demonstrates that inclusive design does not require significantly more funding, but rather a reallocation of priorities toward amenities that serve a broader range of users.

Intervention Inclusive Design Rationale Cost Estimate Phase
Tensile shade sails (3) Weather protection for all users, especially elderly and unhoused individuals $30k–$55k 1
ADA seating with armrests (20) Supports elderly and mobility-limited users $18k–$30k 1
Moveable chairs (40) Flexible seating without hostile design features $10k–$18k 1
Drinking fountain (dual-height) Basic dignity amenity, critical during heat $8k–$15k 1
Pedestrian-scale lighting Supports safe, welcoming nighttime use $20k–$35k 2
Native planted borders Creates welcoming, maintained edges $30k–$50k 2
Bilingual signage Language accessibility (English/Spanish) $7k–$13k 1
Accessible interior path Connects all zones, not just entrances $25k–$45k 2

Total Estimated Cost: $148,000 – $261,000 over approximately 18 months.

The distribution of costs highlights a key argument of this proposal: the most dignity-affirming features are often among the least expensive. The drinking fountain, for example, represents one of the lowest-cost interventions but provides a critical resource for all users, particularly those without access to indoor facilities. Inclusive design is therefore not a question of higher cost, but of intentional allocation.

Human Reflection on AI Use

What AI helped with: AI tools supported rapid visual exploration and iteration. DALL·E was used to generate before-and-after renderings of the plaza redesign. Approximately four images were generated, testing different layouts, seating configurations, and spatial arrangements. These visuals helped communicate atmosphere and test how design ideas might look at a human scale.

However, the initial outputs revealed important limitations. The first generated image primarily depicted elderly and disabled users, which did not reflect the full range of intended users for the plaza. In addition, many generated images showed predominantly white individuals, which does not reflect the actual demographics of Harlem or Hamilton Heights. These biases required active correction through prompt refinement, iteration, and critical selection.

AI Tools and Process: This project used ChatGPT Business as a language model to support writing development, structure the proposal, and refine design arguments. DALL·E was used for image generation. HTML5 and CSS were used to design and build the website. AI was used iteratively, but always as a tool for exploration rather than decision-making.

Specific Human Judgment Decisions:

  • Defining inclusion: The decision to name unhoused individuals as a user group came from understanding that inclusion in public space is incomplete unless it includes the most vulnerable users. This is an ethical design commitment that AI cannot make independently.
  • Rejecting hostile design: The proposal explicitly avoids features such as segmented benches, sloped seating, or deliberately uncomfortable surfaces. This decision came from researching how public space has historically been designed to exclude unhoused individuals, and choosing to take a different position.
  • Community-specific design: The decision that bilingual English/Spanish signage should be a baseline condition — not an added feature — came from understanding that Hamilton Heights is a predominantly Dominican-American neighborhood. This reflects local cultural context that AI does not account for unless explicitly directed.
  • Correcting AI bias: The design process required identifying and correcting bias in generated images, including limited diversity in representation and overly narrow user assumptions. This reinforced the need for human oversight to ensure the project reflects the real community it serves.

What required human judgment: Human decisions defined what inclusion means in this plaza, selected the final concept, interpreted real user needs, and evaluated whether the design supports dignity, accessibility, and shared public use in a meaningful way. While AI supported visualization and iteration, the core design values, priorities, and ethical commitments were entirely human-driven.
